# Historical Hacks: Teaching Students to Think, Not Just Remember

<img src="{{ '/images/projects/historical-hacks.jpg' | url }}" alt="Historical Hacks" class="post-featured-image" />

## The Problem: History Was Being Taught as a List of Facts

When I began developing Lumen's U.S. History course, I noticed something that research confirmed: most students arrive believing that studying history means memorizing dates and events — and most history courses, even well-intentioned ones, reinforce that belief. The content gets covered. The thinking skills don't.

I spent considerable time researching what was actually happening in history education at the college level — reading AHA publications, bottleneck research from Indiana University's History Learning Project, course design literature, and syllabi from community college instructors around the country. What emerged was a clear picture of a systemic gap.

**"History is not a string of facts."** Students who believe their job is to regurgitate dates and events are overwhelmed when instruction revolves around analysis, interrogation, interpretation, and argumentation — operations that are obvious to historians but invisible to students who have never been explicitly taught them.

Indiana University's research identified seven common "bottleneck" areas where history students consistently get stuck: understanding the role of facts, interpreting primary sources, maintaining appropriate emotional distance, understanding the limits of historical actors' knowledge, identifying with people in another time and place, constructing and evaluating arguments, and linking specific details to a broader context. These weren't fringe struggles — they were nearly universal.

The stakes were high. U.S. History is a gateway course, and a failing grade correlates strongly with students leaving college altogether. DFWI (D, F, Withdraw, Incomplete) rates averaged 25% overall and a troubling 42% for Black students. First-generation students, women, and Pell grant recipients were also disproportionately affected. The course wasn't just an academic exercise — it was a retention issue.

**Key insight:** The American Historical Association's revised Tuning Project standards explicitly named historical thinking as a core competency — not a bonus. If the course was going to serve the students who needed it most, it had to teach the skills of historical reasoning explicitly, not assume they would develop on their own.

## The Solution: Embed the Skills Inside the Course

The design response was to build historical thinking directly into the course architecture rather than treat it as supplemental. The result was "Historical Hacks" — a dedicated section at the end of each module that names a specific thinking skill, explains it clearly, connects it to the module's content, and gives students a chance to practice it.

Each Hack was tied to its own learning outcomes and designed to scaffold progressively across the course — starting with foundational skills like analyzing primary sources and building toward more complex operations like constructing historical arguments and practicing civil discourse with opposing viewpoints.

### Sample Hacks Across the Course

- **Module 1 — Analyzing Primary Sources:** HAPPY & OPRY analysis frameworks
- **Module 3 — Historical Empathy:** Understanding systemic racism through Ida B. Wells
- **Module 5 — Bias & Sensationalism:** Identifying bias in historical and modern media
- **Module 7 — The Research Process:** SIFT and CRAAP source evaluation methods
- **Module 9 — Crafting Arguments:** Thesis statements and evidence synthesis
- **Module 15 — Civil Discourse:** Rogerian arguments and opposing viewpoints

The skills chosen weren't arbitrary. Each Hack maps directly to one of the AHA's core historical competency categories: analyzing and interpreting sources, making historical connections, chronological reasoning, and constructing historical arguments. The full arc of the course was designed to take students from "what happened?" to "how do we know, and what does it mean?"

## Years of Iteration — and a Real Constraint

The Hacks were developed alongside the module content and faculty SMEs, grounding each skill in the primary sources and historical examples most relevant to that period. The goal was for the thinking skill to feel inseparable from the content — not like a bolt-on exercise, but like a natural extension of engaging with the material.

**Initial Development:** Extensive needs analysis informed revised global course goals and learning outcomes. Historical Hacks were built into the course architecture from the start — not added later — with their own module-level learning outcomes and primary source connections.

**First Implementation:** Faculty and students responded positively to the Hacks. However, a significant structural limitation became clear: the course platform didn't allow instructors to see student responses to the open-ended Hack activities. Students had no way to submit their thinking, and instructors had no way to know if the Hacks were being used at all. "Students don't do optional" became a familiar refrain.

**2025–2026 Revision:** The current revision preserves the Hacks but splits the design into two layers: a streamlined student-facing version students can engage with independently, and a new set of detailed instructor guide extensions that bring the application into class time — with the instructor as guide and facilitator rather than a passive observer of something happening invisibly online.

**The constraint became a design principle:** If the technology can't close the loop between student thinking and instructor awareness, the design has to. Moving the application into the classroom — where it can be seen, facilitated, and responded to in real time — is a better solution than hoping students engage with open-ended prompts in the dark.

## Outcomes and Honest Reflection

**What worked:** Naming the skills explicitly and connecting them to real primary sources gave students a vocabulary for historical thinking they hadn't had before. Faculty reported that students who engaged with the Hacks were more prepared to discuss and analyze, not just recall.

**What didn't work:** Optional activities in an asynchronous digital environment face an uphill battle. Without accountability structures or instructor visibility, engagement was inconsistent — and the impact was impossible to measure meaningfully.

**The design response:** Rather than abandoning the framework, the 2025–26 revision meets students where the accountability actually exists — in the classroom, with an instructor present. The Hacks become the foundation; the in-class activities become the application.

**The bigger picture:** This project is a long game. Shifting how students understand what history is — from a collection of facts to an interpretive practice — requires consistent, scaffolded exposure across an entire course. The framework is sound; the delivery keeps improving.

### Design Reflection

The Historical Hacks project taught me that good instructional design requires more than well-designed content — it requires understanding the full ecosystem in which that content lives. The skills were right. The research was solid. But a beautifully designed activity that students never see, or that instructors can't respond to, doesn't achieve its purpose.

The most valuable thing this project reinforced is the importance of designing for the constraints that actually exist, not the ideal conditions you wish existed. Technology limitations, instructor bandwidth, and student behavior patterns are all part of the design problem — not obstacles to good design.
