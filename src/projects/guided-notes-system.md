# Scaffolded Guided Notes for Equitable Learning

<img src="{{ '/images/projects/guided-notes-system.jpg' | url }}" alt="Guided Notes System" class="post-featured-image" />

## The Problem: Equity Starts at the Starting Line

Lumen Learning's mission is to support unprecedented learning for all students. After years of working closely with faculty, students, and course data, I kept noticing the same tension: our courseware was strong, but students arrived with very different levels of preparation. High-quality content alone doesn't close that gap — students who already know how to learn have a structural advantage, and in a fully digital environment, that gap widens further.

Some colleagues argued that note-taking is a skill students should develop independently. I agreed — but I also believed that **withholding scaffolding in the name of skill-building is not equity. It's a barrier.** The goal was never to remove the thinking. It was to give every learner a common baseline to think from.

**Learner Gap:** Students entered with uneven study strategies. Digital-only content offered no physical touchpoint or organizational scaffold.

**Equity Gap:** Students from under-resourced backgrounds were most disadvantaged by the absence of structured support materials.

**Alignment Gap:** Instructor-written exams sometimes diverged from courseware emphasis, leaving students without a clear map of priorities.

**Physical Gap:** Student feedback revealed a persistent desire for something tangible — material they could print, hold, and write in.

## The Solution: Simple, Flexible, and Printable

The format decision was deliberate: Google Docs, not a proprietary platform. Google Docs are universally accessible, easily copied and modified by students, and printable with no special login required. The sophistication was in the design thinking, not the delivery mechanism.

Each document followed the module outline exactly, organized by section headers matching the course's "Learn It" pages. The format blended fill-in-the-blank vocabulary, hierarchical structural outlines, and occasional open-ended reflection prompts — giving students a map of how concepts related without removing the work of engaging with the material.

> "With everything being online and so intangible, students said they were still craving something they could touch and write in."

The first set of guided notes was created entirely by hand. I worked through every module myself — reading the content, building each outline from scratch, and making deliberate decisions about what belonged and what didn't. It was time-intensive, but that firsthand engagement with the material shaped the quality of what I built.

When ChatGPT became available, I developed custom GPTs trained on the course content and my formatting specifications to generate new module drafts at scale. This dramatically reduced development time while preserving the instructional approach I'd established manually. I later documented the workflow and trained my team to use it, extending the system across the full course catalog.

**One key revision:** Early iterations were too granular. Students would sometimes hunt for a single missing word rather than engaging with the concept. After observing this in my own classroom at Davidson College, I shifted toward more holistic outlines that invited synthesis over transcription — a small change with a meaningful impact on how students actually used the notes.

## From Pilot to Catalog Standard

Guided notes launched in Intro Psychology and spread quickly. Students printed them and brought them to class. Faculty reported stronger student orientation in discussions. Lumen's sales team recognized them as a differentiator with institutional partners. Based on that reception, guided notes became standard protocol across the catalog.

**What worked:** Students who used printed notes arrived with a framework and could focus on deepening understanding rather than capturing basics. The physical artifact increased engagement and a sense of ownership.

**What I changed:** Overly specific prompts led students to disengage while searching for exact answers. Shifting to holistic outlines improved engagement and reduced the "word search" behavior noticeably.

### Design Reflection

This project reinforced something I believe deeply: the best instructional solutions are often the simplest ones. A Google Doc outperformed any proprietary tool — because it was accessible, flexible, printable, and student-controlled.

It also validated the equity argument. Scaffolding is not the opposite of rigor. Done well, it's what makes rigor accessible to everyone — not just students who already know how to learn.
